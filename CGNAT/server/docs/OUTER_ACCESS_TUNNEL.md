# Outer Access Tunnel

## Purpose

This document defines the role and requirements of the outer access tunnel
between the CGNAT ISP HEAD END and the CGNAT HEAD END.

This tunnel is the first layer of the CGNAT design. It is not the customer
service VPN itself. Its job is to provide authenticated transport into the
platform so that downstream inner VPN traffic can be carried and steered.

## Functional Role

The outer access tunnel must:

- originate from the CGNAT ISP HEAD END
- terminate on the CGNAT HEAD END
- authenticate using certificates
- tolerate unknown, changing, or CGNATed public source identity
- carry the downstream inner S2S VPN traffic generated by Customer Devices

## Non-Role Clarification

The outer access tunnel does not:

- replace the inner VPN
- terminate the customer's service identity
- remove the need for backend VPN head-end steering
- use the same trust model as the inner VPN

It is an access tunnel, not the final customer service tunnel.

## Authentication Model

### Required Behavior

Authentication for the outer access tunnel must be based on certificates rather
than fixed peer IP matching.

That is required because the source public identity on the ISP side may be:

- unknown ahead of time
- dynamic
- behind CGNAT

### Design Intent

The platform should trust the CGNAT ISP HEAD END because it presents valid
certificate identity, not because it arrives from a predictable address.

### Demo PKI Model for Scenario 1

For Scenario 1, the current demo assumption is:

- the CGNAT HEAD END runs a local CA
- customer-side outer tunnel certificates are issued from that local CA
- this is a demo and test trust model only

Production direction is different:

- certificate issuance should later move to a third-party or enterprise CA
- the local CA on the CGNAT HEAD END should not be treated as the production
  end state

## Identity Expectations

### Outer Identity

The outer identity is the identity of the CGNAT ISP HEAD END as authenticated
by certificate.

This identity is used to:

- allow the access tunnel to form
- associate the tunnel with the correct CGNAT service context
- establish a transport path into the CGNAT HEAD END

### Inner Identity

The inner VPN identity belongs to the downstream Customer Devices and must be
treated separately.

That means the outer-tunnel certificate does not replace:

- customer keying
- customer inside identity
- customer service identity

## Transport Expectations

Once the outer tunnel is established, it must be able to carry:

- inner IKE negotiation
- inner IPsec traffic
- any supporting traffic needed to deliver the inner VPN to backend VPN head
  ends

The transport model should be documented and tested so we know exactly which
traffic is allowed to traverse the outer tunnel and how it is classified at the
CGNAT HEAD END.

## Scenario Patterns

### Scenario 1: Per-Customer Outer Tunnel

In Scenario 1, the outer tunnel is formed directly by the customer-side device.

This means:

- the customer device may also be the logical CGNAT ISP-side endpoint
- the outer tunnel is 1:1 with the inner customer tunnel
- the outer tunnel is expected to run in NAT-T mode because the customer is
  behind CGN

### Scenario 2: Shared ISP Outer Tunnel

In Scenario 2, the outer tunnel is formed once by an ISP/interconnect gateway
and carries multiple downstream inner customer tunnels.

This means:

- the outer tunnel is n:1 with respect to the inner tunnels
- the outer tunnel capability matrix may be broader than Scenario 1
- the outer tunnel may be NAT-T or non-NAT-T
- the outer tunnel may be IKEv1 or IKEv2 depending on interconnect realities
- initiator/responder behavior may vary by provider design

## Independence Rule

The outer tunnel mode must not be treated as a forced property of the inner
tunnel.

Examples:

- outer tunnel may be NAT-T while inner tunnel remains non-NAT-T
- outer tunnel may be provider-owned while inner tunnel remains customer-owned

That independence is especially important in Scenario 1 where the customer is
behind CGN but the inner tunnel still targets the backend service in a way that
may not detect NAT on the inner layer.

## Placement Constraints

The outer access tunnel must be designed against the approved placement model:

- CGNAT HEAD END only in `subnet-04a6b7f3a3855d438`
- CGNAT ISP HEAD END spanning:
  - `subnet-04a6b7f3a3855d438`
  - `subnet-0e6ae1d598e08d002`

All related addressing and interface assumptions must be variable-driven.

## Operational Requirements

The outer access tunnel design should support:

- clear up/down state visibility
- clear certificate/authentication failure visibility
- clean separation between outer-tunnel failures and inner-VPN failures
- rollback-safe test deployment behavior

## Observability Requirements

At minimum, the design should eventually expose enough information to answer:

1. Did the CGNAT ISP HEAD END authenticate successfully?
2. Did the outer tunnel establish successfully?
3. Is the CGNAT HEAD END currently treating the tunnel as usable transport?
4. Is inner VPN traffic reaching the CGNAT HEAD END through that tunnel?
5. Can failures be distinguished between certificate, transport, and inner-VPN
   causes?

## Open Design Questions

These questions are intentionally left open for later design phases:

- What exact software/runtime terminates the outer tunnel on the CGNAT HEAD
  END?
- How is outer-tunnel certificate identity mapped to service context?
- What minimal policy is allowed across the outer tunnel before inner-VPN
  steering logic takes over?
- What monitoring signal should drive outer-tunnel health?

## Acceptance Criteria for This Document

This document is considered complete enough for the current phase when:

- the outer tunnel is clearly separated from the inner VPN in both role and
  trust model
- certificate authentication is identified as mandatory
- the no-fixed-peer-IP requirement is explicit
- the placement constraints are aligned with the approved subnet model
- later implementation work can reference this document without re-opening the
  basic purpose of the outer tunnel
